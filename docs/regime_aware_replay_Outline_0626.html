Presentation Brief | Regime-Aware Replay for Financial RL

From Regime-Aware Portfolio RL to Regime-Aware Experience Replay

Goal for tonight: explain the research gap clearly, show what we have already built, and propose a validation path that can convince a reviewer that the replay mechanism is useful.

01

One-Sentence Positioning

We are not trying to claim that markets have regimes or that regime-aware portfolio RL is new. Our narrower question is about replay.

Research question: When a financial RL agent trains from a replay buffer that mixes multiple market regimes, can regime-aware and discrepancy-aware sampling reduce stale experience reuse and improve post-shift adaptation, risk control, or out-of-sample stability?

The Problem

Replay buffers improve sample efficiency, but financial data are non-stationary. A transition from a bull market may not be equally useful in a high-volatility selloff.

The Method

Use external regime labels plus DEER-style Q-discrepancy to decide which historical transitions are still relevant after a regime boundary.

Expected Outcomes

We expect better post-boundary adaptation, lower replay mismatch, cleaner priority behavior, and more stable risk metrics such as drawdown and turnover.

02

Two Anchor Papers and the Gap

ReCAP motivates the financial-regime setting. DEER motivates the replay-priority mechanism. Our gap is at their intersection.

Paper What It Does Why It Matters for Us Remaining Gap
ReCAP
Regime-Adaptive Continual Learning for Portfolio Management		 ReCAP is like giving the agent several regime-specific strategy memories. When the market changes, it does not train from scratch; it decides which historical policy memories should be reused and mixed for the current market. Its form is close to our problem because it is also regime-aware financial RL. We can use its experimental design as a reference for validating our proposed replay model. It is mainly policy-level adaptation. It does not isolate replay-buffer contamination or transition-level sampling as the main object of study.
DEER
Sample Efficient Experience Replay in Non-Stationary Environments		 DEER says that after the environment changes, a high-error old sample may not be useful; it may simply be stale. So it compares old and new value estimates and changes replay priority accordingly. It provides the core idea for regime-aware replay: replay should depend not only on TD-error, but also on whether a transition still matches the current environment. It is a general control paper, not a financial-market paper. It does not study market regimes, portfolio backtests, transaction costs, drawdown, or turnover.
Proposed gap: prior work studies either financial regime-aware policy adaptation or general non-stationary replay. We study financial regime-aware experience replay: how replay sampling should change when market regimes shift.
03

What Our Current MVP Looks Like

Already Built or In Progress

  • Three regime labelers: rule-based, HMM, and ReCAP-inspired CUSUM.
  • DQN comparison framework: online, uniform replay, PER, and DEER-style replay.
  • Daily market/portfolio environment with transaction costs.
  • Diagnostics for portfolio value, drawdown, turnover, mismatch rate, TD-error, priority, DoE, and S-score.

DEER-Style Mechanism

  • External regime label change creates a new boundary.
  • At the boundary, freeze a reference Q network snapshot as the old market belief.
  • After the agent starts learning the new regime, compare the new Q estimate with the frozen old Q estimate.
  • DoE(s,a) = |Q_new(s,a) - Q_old(s,a)|. Intuitively, this measures how much the value of the same decision changed after the regime shift.
  • Use a transition-count S schedule: high DoE emphasis right after a boundary, then gradually return to TD-error refinement.
Transition Type Replay Logic Intuition
Pre-boundary transitions Lower priority when DoE is high. If an old transition produces a very different Q estimate now, it may be stale under the current market regime.
Post-boundary transitions Mix TD-error and DoE using S. Right after a shift, focus on transitions that reveal the new environment; later, return to normal TD-error learning.
04

Validation Principle: Why Should This Mechanism Be Useful?

We need two layers of evidence: portfolio backtest results and replay-mechanism diagnostics.

Layer A: Portfolio-Level Results

This is the main evidence because financial reviewers care about whether the mechanism improves trading behavior under realistic costs.

  • Final portfolio value / cumulative return.
  • Sharpe ratio and Sortino ratio.
  • Maximum drawdown and drawdown duration.
  • Turnover and transaction-cost robustness.
  • Post-regime-shift recovery speed.

Layer B: Mechanism-Level Diagnostics

This is what makes the project more than a trading-system comparison.

  • Replay mismatch rate: current regime vs sampled transition regime.
  • Sample age and regime distribution inside batches.
  • Priority distribution before/after regime switches.
  • Correlation between mismatch and TD-error, drawdown, or reward instability.
  • DoE behavior around detected regime boundaries.
Important framing: We should not only report return. If return is noisy, but the method reliably reduces replay contamination and improves post-shift risk/recovery metrics, the mechanism is still publishable as a focused financial RL contribution.

Backtest Design Inspired by ReCAP

ReCAP's experiments are useful because they evaluate across multiple asset universes, multiple baselines, transaction costs, risk metrics, ablations, and regime adaptation metrics.

Experiment Roadmap and Priority

The order matters. We should first prove the trading environment and baseline are meaningful, then test whether replay behavior changes, then test whether it improves portfolio outcomes.

Priority Experiment Question Answered Success Signal
Main 1 DQN replay backtest on DOW30 or ETF universe with 5-10 seeds. Does DEER-style or regime-aware replay improve portfolio behavior under transaction costs? Better median MDD/recovery/Sharpe or final value than PER/uniform, without extreme turnover.
Main 2 Replay contamination diagnostics around regime boundaries. Does standard replay sample stale or mismatched transitions after shifts? Uniform/PER have higher mismatch; our method lowers mismatch and changes priority composition as designed.
Main 3 Post-shift event study. Does the method adapt faster after regime changes? Lower drawdown in the first 20/40/60 trading days after a boundary; faster recovery to pre-shift rolling performance.
Ablation No DoE, no S schedule, random boundary, shuffled labels. Which component actually matters? True boundary + DoE + S should beat random/shuffled/no-DoE versions on mechanism metrics and at least one risk metric.
Robustness Compare rule-based, HMM, and ReCAP-CUSUM labels. Is the result dependent on one labeler? Qualitatively similar replay behavior across at least two labelers.
Robustness Transaction-cost sensitivity: 0, 5, 10 bps. Does improvement survive realistic trading frictions? Risk/recovery improvements remain under 10 bps; turnover is not the hidden driver.
Fallback Mechanism-only paper angle. What if portfolio returns are too noisy? Focus contribution on replay contamination diagnosis, controlled synthetic market, and financial diagnostics.
07

Specific Experiments to Explain Better Samples

These are the experiments that make the mechanism believable even if raw return is unstable.

Sample Quality Test

  • For each minibatch, log regime distribution, sample age, TD-error, DoE, and realized next-period reward.
  • Compare whether our sampler selects more current-relevant or low-staleness transitions after boundaries.
  • Report batch-level statistics before and after each regime shift.

Counterfactual Replay Test

  • Keep the same DQN, same environment, same seeds, and same training steps.
  • Only change the replay sampler: uniform, PER, sliding-window, and DEER-style.
  • If results differ, the difference is more attributable to replay rather than model architecture.

Boundary Event Study

  • Align all detected regime boundaries at day 0.
  • Plot average MDD, reward, turnover, and portfolio value from day -20 to day +60.
  • The desired effect is smaller post-boundary loss and faster recovery.

Negative Controls

  • Use random boundaries with the same number of switches.
  • Shuffle regime labels while preserving label frequencies.
  • If performance remains the same, then our labels are not carrying useful regime information.
